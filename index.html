Project Overview: The incidence of type 1 diabetes (T1D) is increasing globally. Recent reports suggest that by 2025, 18.7 million people in Sub-Saharan Africa will be affected by the condition. In SSA, T1D is most frequently diagnosed in adolescents, or “youth” between 13-18 years old. Technologies are available to help youth manage T1D (e.g., mobile apps). However, these interventions are typically designed for populations in high-income countries (e.g., United States) and rarely account for African youth’s needs and context.

To address this problem, the National Institutes of Health (NIH) funded researchers at Michigan State University to use of human-centered design (HCD) to develop an intervention for and with Kenyan youth. In collaboration with 24 youth, their caregivers, doctors at government health clinics, as well as the Kenyan Diabetes Management and Information Center (DMI), Rural Outreach Africa and a local design team, a paper based T1D diary was developed.
